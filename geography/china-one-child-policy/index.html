1949 — year zero

The People’s Republic of China is founded with a population of around 540 million. The early decades are turbulent: a series of natural disasters — earthquakes, hurricanes and floods — brings famine, and for a time deaths outnumber births and the population falls.

The Great Leap Forward

Chairman Mao Zedong launches a programme to make China strong again, transforming the economy from agriculture towards industry. The Great Leap Forward reverses the decline, on the principle that “a large population gives a strong nation”.

Key term — pro-natalist: a policy that encourages births, to raise the birth rate.

The boom

Chinese population experts had suggested an optimum population of around 700 million. China races past it: during the Cultural Revolution the population grows by 55 million every three years — from 540 million in 1949 to 940 million by 1976. Watch the gauge.

The question changes

By the mid-1970s the question facing China’s planners is no longer how to grow. It is how to feed everyone. Before going on, prove you can read the demographic data behind this story.

Feeding a nation

China must feed 22% of the world’s population from less than 7% of the world’s arable land. In the 1960s an estimated 42 million people die in famines, and the government has to acknowledge that rising fertility is creating pressure on the balance of resources. This imbalance between population and resources is the heart of the case study.

Approaching one billion

Despite the mass famines, the population keeps climbing — quickly approaching 1 billion. A new, firmer approach to population control is required.

First steps — the 1970s

State family planning programmes arrive, such as ‘Wan-xi-shao’, which encourages later marriages and longer gaps between children.

1979 — the One-Child Policy

China adopts a sweeping anti-natalist policy — population control imposed in a way few other countries could replicate. Its main aim: reduce the number of children born per woman. It is also intended to limit future demands for water and other resources, and to raise standards of living.

Key term — anti-natalist: a policy that discourages births, to lower the birth rate.
